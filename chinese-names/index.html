A #WIP Guide to Chinese Names

Do you know Zhang is actually pronounced as "John"?
Here's a quick guide of how to read Mandarin names in pinyin. Yes, just names, not learning the language (yet)

General Rules 一般规则

It's an artificial phonetics system designed with minimum redundancy

  • There's a 1-to-1 mapping of each sound and spelling. Aka if they're spelled differently, they sound different
  • There are no silent letters

A name / last name usually breaks into 1-2 syllables of consonant + vowel.

  • Consonant is optional. And when used in English, tones (accents) are dropped.
  • Here's an exhaustive list of all consonant/vowel pairs in Mandarin

It's the effort that counts!

  • You may want to get the obvious ones right, like Li is not "Lie"
  • Don't sweat too much about a sound that's foreign to English. Actually, j,q,x don't even exist in Cantonese.

Special Notes

In the digital age however, ü is almost always just written as u.

  • As a rule of thumb, when after j,q,x or in ue, u is acutally prounced ü
  • Maybe the only serious problem this simplification causes is when paired with l,n. As both Lü and Lu are very common last names, the former is sometimes also spelled as Lyu, Luu, or Lv.

Unfortunately, it's not always obvious where a syllable begins/ ends

  • E.g. Xi'an vs. Xian, that's why you sometimes see the '.

Special Notes

In zi,ci,si,ri,zhi,chi,shi ("A Lil Tricky"), the i only serves as a placeholder

  • Aka the full syllable sounds exactly as if there are no vowels, if that makes sense, but longer. (You still need this vowel to mark the tones/accents though)

Somehow there's this rule that you can't begin a syllable with i, u, ü. Instead, use yi, wu, yu.

  • This doesn't change how they sound.
